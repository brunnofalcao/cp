You sleep the recommended hours. You go to bed early, avoid screens, do "everything right." And still wake up as if you had carried weight all night.

Waking up tired every day is not a personality trait. It is a symptom — and sleep is one of the most neglected axes in the investigation of complex cases.

## Sleeping is not the same as resting

Restorative sleep depends on architecture: organized cycles of light, deep and REM sleep, with enough continuity for the body to perform its functions — memory consolidation, immune and hormonal regulation, cerebral metabolic clearance.

When that architecture is fragmented, you can "sleep 8 hours" and rest 4. And the treacherous part: most of the time, you don't notice the awakenings.

## What fragments sleep

- **Obstructive sleep apnea:** breathing pauses forcing micro-awakenings, dozens or hundreds of times a night. It is not exclusive to overweight men who snore: in women it may present as insomnia, fatigue and morning headache, without prominent snoring.
- **Periodic limb movements** and restless legs syndrome
- **Chronic pain:** pain and poor sleep feed each other — pain fragments sleep, and fragmented sleep lowers the pain threshold
- **Reflux, nasal congestion and autonomic arousals**, frequent in conditions like dysautonomia and MCAS

## The connection with complex cases

In the investigation of chronic fatigue, brain fog, Long COVID, EDS and MCAS, assessing sleep is not an accessory — it is structural. Fragmented sleep amplifies pain, worsens dysautonomia, aggravates cognitive symptoms and undermines any treatment. Ignoring this axis is trying to fill a leaking bucket.

## How it is investigated

1. **Detailed sleep history:** schedules, awakenings, snoring, nighttime behavior (a bed partner's report is gold)
2. **Validated scales** for sleepiness and sleep quality
3. **Polysomnography** when indicated: the exam that records breathing, oxygenation, brain activity and movements through the night
4. **Correlation with the full picture:** sleep as a piece of the system, not an isolated complaint

> Sleep is not a pause in life. It is a third of it — and it is often where the investigation finds the missing piece.

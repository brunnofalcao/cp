The infection is over. Your tests "are normal." But you never went back to being who you were: energy runs out before the day does, yesterday's effort sends today the bill, and your mind feels covered in fog.

This has a name, a scientific definition and an investigative path.

## What Long COVID actually is

The World Health Organization defines Long COVID as the continuation or development of new symptoms three months after SARS-CoV-2 infection, lasting at least two months, with no other diagnostic explanation.

The most frequent symptoms in the literature:

- **Fatigue**, disproportionate and persistent
- **Post-exertional malaise:** significant worsening after physical or mental activity
- **Sleep disturbances** and non-restorative sleep
- **Shortness of breath** and exercise intolerance
- **Brain fog:** difficulty with concentration and memory
- **Palpitations and dizziness**, especially when standing

## Why it happens — what science is investigating

Long COVID is not a mystery without clues. The main lines of evidence point to mechanisms that can coexist:

1. **Immune system dysfunction** and persistent low-grade inflammation
2. **Dysautonomia:** dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system, which controls heart rate, blood pressure and digestion — frequently as POTS
3. **Mast cell activation**, with allergy-like, multisystem symptoms
4. **Microvascular and tissue perfusion changes**
5. **Viral persistence or viral fragments** in some tissues

That is why treating Long COVID as "just tiredness" or "just anxiety" fails: the condition is multisystemic and demands a matching investigation.

## The most common mistake: normal basic tests = discharge

A normal blood count, TSH and vitamin D do not rule out Long COVID. Proper investigation considers the detailed history, the symptom pattern (especially post-exertional malaise), sleep assessment, autonomic testing when indicated, and screening for comorbidities the infection may have triggered — such as dysautonomia and mast cell activation.

## What you can record before your consultation

- Timeline: when the infection happened, when each symptom appeared
- What worsens and what relieves symptoms (exertion, heat, food, stress)
- Sleep pattern and energy levels throughout the day
- Resting and standing heart rate, if you can measure them

These data turn an appointment into an investigation.

> Your symptoms are real. Science already recognizes this — and correct investigation starts with taking you seriously.
